The Okapi

Physical Description

The Okapi is a quadrupedal mammal with patterns similar to that of a zebra. The Okapi has an average height of 4.9ft and an average body length of 8.2ft and weighs in the range of 440-770 pounds. Typical characteristics include a long neck and large, flexible ears.

Its coat colors can range from chocolate to reddish-brown, with white stripes and rings on the legs and ankles.The males have short horn-like protrusions on their head called ossicones that are around 5.9 inches in length while the females have hair whorls.

Habitat

The okapi is endemic to the Democratic Republic of the Congo, where it occurs north and east of the Congo River. It ranges from the Maiko National Park northward to the Ituri rainforest, then through the river basins of the Rubi, Lake Tele, and Ebola to the west and the Ubangi River further north. Smaller populations exist west and south of the Congo River. It is also common in the Wamba and Epulu areas. It is extinct in Uganda.[3]

The okapi inhabits canopy forests at altitudes of 500–1,500 m (1,600–4,900 ft). It occasionally uses seasonally inundated areas, but does not occur in gallery forests, swamp forests, and habitats disturbed by human settlements. In the wet season, it visits rocky inselbergs that offer forage uncommon elsewhere. Results of research conducted in the late 1980s in a mixed Cynometra forest indicated that the okapi population density averaged 0.53 animals per square kilometre.[3]

Diet

Okapis are herbivorous, meaning they feed on solely on plant matter. They are unique in the Ituri Forest as they are the only known mammal that feeds solely on understory vegetation, where they use their 18-inch tongues to selectively browse for suitable plants. Their tounge is also used for perosnal upkeep and grooming.

They are known to feed on over 100 species of plants, some of which are posionious to humans and other animals. Staple foods comprise shrubs and lianas

Legal Status and Protection

The IUCN classified the okapi as endangered in 2013 and still currently does, and it's fully protected under Congolese law. Wikipedia says, "The Okapi Conservation Project, established in 1987, works towards the conservation of the okapi... In November 2011, the White Oak Conservation center and Jacksonville Zoo and Gardens hosted an international meeting of the Okapi Species Survival Plan and the Okapi European Endangered Species Programme at Jacksonville, which was attended by representatives from zoos from the US, Europe, and Japan. The aim was to discuss the management of captive okapis and arrange support for okapi conservation. Many zoos in North America and Europe currently have okapis in captivity."[4]

Around 100 Okapis are in AZA zoos, which are zoos that are dedicated to, amoung other things, conservation of animals. Sources[I] say that, "The okapi population is managed in America by the AZA's Species Survival Plan, a breeding program that works to ensure genetic diversity in the captive population of endangered animals."

Other Facts

  • Although the okapi has striped markings reminiscent of zebras, it is most closely related to the giraffe.
  • The okapi and the giraffe are the only living members of the family Giraffidae.
  • The stripes it has are a form of camouflage that helps them blend in while in partial sunlight.
  • In Asia, only two zoos in Japan exhibit okapis; Ueno Zoo in Tokyo and Zoorasia in Yokohama.
